Breakout Strategy — QQQ

Turtle Trading with Choppiness Index & Volume Filters

Strategy Logic

This strategy is a long/short trend-following system built on the Donchian Channel breakout framework, originally popularized by the Turtle Trading experiment of the 1980s. The core idea is simple: when a market makes a new multi-week high or low, it is signaling the potential start of a new directional trend — and the strategy takes a position in that direction to ride the move. A long position of 75 shares is entered when price closes above its 20-day highest high, and a short position of 75 shares is entered when price closes below its 20-day lowest low. To avoid entering on false breakouts — which are the primary weakness of any breakout system — two filters must be satisfied simultaneously before any trade is taken: the Choppiness Index must confirm the market is in a trending rather than choppy regime, and volume must be above its 20-day average to confirm genuine participation behind the move. Once entered, positions are managed with a stop-loss at 3 times the Average True Range from entry, a Donchian-based exit when price reverses through the opposite channel boundary, and a hard timeout of 20 trading days as a maximum holding period. The backtest rolls forward sequentially through time — each decision only uses data that was available on that day, with no look-ahead into future prices.

Asset Selection

The strategy was applied to QQQ (Invesco Nasdaq-100 ETF). Several candidate assets were considered, including SPY (S&P 500), IWM (Russell 2000), and GLD (Gold ETF). QQQ was selected for three reasons. First, it has the highest average daily dollar volume of any US equity ETF, which means entry and exit on 75-share lots can be executed with minimal slippage. Second, the Nasdaq-100 has historically exhibited strong momentum characteristics — when it trends, it tends to trend decisively, which is the regime a Donchian breakout system is designed to exploit. Third, unlike individual stocks, QQQ is diversified enough that single-name events (earnings, FDA decisions) do not distort the breakout signals. SPY was rejected because its lower volatility produces fewer and smaller breakouts. IWM was rejected because its higher mean-reversion tendency makes trend-following less reliable. GLD was considered but rejected because ShinyBroker’s data coverage for commodity ETFs over the 2-year window was less consistent.

Breakout Detection Function

The breakout detection function detect_breakout() evaluates a single price bar and returns one of three values: +1 (enter long), -1 (enter short), or 0 (no trade). It takes three inputs: the current bar’s data, the Donchian lookback period N, and whether the most recently completed trade was profitable.

The function checks conditions in this order:

Condition 1 · Donchian Channel Breakout

The current bar’s closing price must exceed the highest high of the previous 20 bars (long signal) or fall below the lowest low of the previous 20 bars (short signal). The channel is always calculated on lagged data — today’s bar is never included in its own channel calculation, preventing any look-ahead bias.

Filter 1 · Choppiness Index < 65

The Choppiness Index, developed by E.W. Dreiss using Benoit Mandelbrot’s fractal geometry, measures whether a price series is behaving like a directional trend or random noise. It is calculated as: 100 × log10(sum of ATR over N periods / (N-period high − N-period low)) / log10(N). Values above 61.8 indicate a choppy, consolidating market where breakouts statistically fail and whipsaws dominate. We use a threshold of 65 to allow a small buffer. If CHOP ≥ 65 the signal is suppressed entirely.

Filter 2 · Volume Confirmation

Today’s volume must exceed its 20-day simple moving average. A breakout on below-average volume reflects thin liquidity rather than genuine directional conviction. Real breakouts — the kind that produce sustained trends — attract above-average participation. This filter rejects low-conviction signals before a position is entered.

Assumptions & Replication Notes

Data source: IBKR Trader Workstation via ShinyBroker. Regular Trading Hours only (RTH=True). Daily OHLCV bars. 2-year history fetched from April 2024 to April 2026 — 501 trading days.

Entry execution: Signals are generated at the close of day T. The position is entered at the open of day T+1. This is conservative and realistic — no same-bar entry.

Stop-loss execution: The stop is treated as a hard intraday level. If the day’s low (for longs) or high (for shorts) crosses the stop price, the trade exits at the stop price on that day.

Commissions & slippage: Not modelled. At 75 shares of QQQ (a highly liquid ETF with sub-cent spreads), the impact on a strategy holding positions for 12+ days on average is negligible relative to the trade P&L magnitudes observed.

Look-ahead bias prevention: All indicator lookbacks use .shift(1) — today’s bar is never used in calculating today’s signal. The backtest loop is strictly sequential, processing one day at a time.

Walk-forward approach: The backtest rolls forward through time day by day. Each signal is generated using only data available up to and including the prior trading day. There is no training/test split optimization applied — N=20 is fixed based on the classic Turtle Trading parameter, keeping the strategy honest and avoiding curve-fitting to this specific dataset.

Trade Outcome Analysis

Every trade is classified into one of three outcomes. The timeout period is 20 trading days. The stop-loss is set at 3 × ATR(14) below entry for longs and above entry for shorts.

Success Trade closed profitably — either via timeout or Donchian exit in profit Stop-loss 3×ATR stop triggered before a profitable exit could occur Timeout 20-day holding limit reached, position exited at market close

Of the 8 trades: 4 were stopped out, 3 were successful timeouts, and 1 was an unsuccessful timeout. Notably, every profitable trade was a timeout — the Donchian exit signal never triggered on a winning trade. This suggests the 20-day channel exit is too slow to capture gains before mean-reversion occurs.

Analysis & Commentary

Did the strategy work?

No — the strategy lost 4.73% over the backtest period while QQQ itself appreciated significantly. However the absolute dollar loss was modest ($4,730 on $100,000) and the maximum drawdown of 6.28% indicates that capital was largely preserved. The strategy spent most of the period in cash, which limited both upside participation and downside risk.

Why did the losing trades lose?

All four losing trades were stopped out within 2 to 15 days. The structural problem is that entry occurs the morning after the breakout signal, by which point the initial explosive move has already occurred. What follows is often a short-term mean-reversion as traders take profits off the breakout, which hits the stop before the real trend has a chance to develop. The two largest losses — August 2024 and April 2025 — both occurred during sudden volatility spikes driven by macro events (the yen carry trade unwind and the tariff shock respectively). In both cases QQQ made a sharp directional move that generated a signal, then immediately reversed as the event stabilized. These are regime-change episodes where trend-following signals produce false positives.

Why did the winning trades win?

All three profitable trades were held for the full 20-day timeout. The strategy never found a clean Donchian exit on a winner — it simply held long enough for the trend to accumulate gains. The implication is that the edge, when it exists, comes from patience and position-holding rather than a precise exit mechanism.

What does the alpha/beta comparison show?

Near-zero beta versus both QQQ and SPY confirms that the strategy’s returns are structurally independent of general market direction. This is expected for a long/short system where longs and shorts partially offset each other’s market exposure. Near-zero alpha means no excess returns are being generated above what the mechanical rules produce. The strategy is not benefiting from luck or market direction — its performance, positive or negative, is entirely a function of its own rules.

Why is breakout trading harder on equities than commodities?

The original Turtle Trading system was designed for commodity futures — crude oil, gold, currencies. These markets trend more persistently because supply and demand imbalances take months or years to resolve. Equity indices like QQQ mean-revert on short timescales because earnings, valuations, and institutional rebalancing constantly pull prices toward fundamental value. The Choppiness Index filter removes many of these false signals, but the 2024-2026 QQQ regime — characterized by sharp policy-driven spikes followed by rapid recoveries — was particularly hostile to trend-following logic. The market moved in ways that looked like breakouts but resolved within days rather than weeks.

How would you improve the model?

The most promising improvement would be a volatility regime filter: skip all signals when realized volatility over the past 5 days is more than 2 standard deviations above its 60-day mean. This targets exactly the spike-and-reverse environment that produced the two largest losses, using only information observable before entry — no look-ahead bias. A second improvement would be entry refinement: rather than entering at next-day open, wait for an intraday pullback of 0.5×ATR back toward the breakout level before entering. This reduces the risk of chasing an exhausted move and directly widens the effective stop distance without changing the stop placement logic.
