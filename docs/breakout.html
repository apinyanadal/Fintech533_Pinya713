Breakout Strategy — QQQ

Turtle Trading with Choppiness Index & Volume Filters

Strategy Overview

This page documents a systematic long/short breakout strategy applied to QQQ (Invesco Nasdaq-100 ETF), built on the foundation of the legendary Turtle Trading System developed by Richard Dennis in the 1980s, and enhanced with two modern filters to reduce whipsaw losses.

Strategy Logic

The strategy identifies moments when QQQ’s price decisively breaks out of a multi-week trading range and enters a position to ride the emerging trend.

Entry signal: A Donchian Channel breakout. A close above the highest high of the previous 20 bars triggers a long entry the next morning at the open. A close below the lowest low triggers a short entry. All lookbacks use .shift(1) so today’s bar never influences its own signal.

Two filters gate every signal:

Filter 1 · Choppiness Index < 65

Developed by E.W. Dreiss using fractal geometry, this indicator measures whether price is behaving like a directional trend (low values) or random noise (high values). Above 65 means choppy regime — all signals skipped.

Filter 2 · Volume > 1.0x 20-Day Average

A breakout on below-average volume has no institutional participation behind it. Volume must exceed its 20-day average before any signal is acted on.

Exit rules in priority order: Stop-loss at 3xATR(14), Donchian exit when close crosses opposite 20-day channel, Timeout after 20 trading days at market close.

Asset Selection

QQQ was chosen for its extreme liquidity, strong trend-following characteristics during sustained moves, and tradability in both directions — experiencing genuine downtrends in 2022 and early 2025 as well as strong uptrends.

Analysis & Commentary

Did the strategy work?

Measured purely by return, no. The strategy lost 4.73% over the backtest period while QQQ itself appreciated significantly. However the loss was small in absolute terms and the maximum drawdown of 6.28% reflects a strategy that mostly sat in cash rather than taking large directional bets. Annual volatility of 5.33% is well below QQQ’s own volatility, meaning the strategy was capital-preserving even when unprofitable.

Why did the losing trades lose?

All four losing trades were stopped out within 2 to 15 days. The structural problem is that the strategy enters the morning after the breakout signal, by which point the explosive move has already occurred. What follows is often a short-term mean-reversion as traders take profits, which hits the stop before the real trend develops. The two largest losses — August 2024 and April 2025 — occurred during sudden volatility spikes (the yen carry unwind and the tariff shock). Both triggered breakout signals that immediately reversed as markets stabilized.

Why did the winning trades win?

All three profitable trades were held for the full 20-day timeout. The strategy never found a clean Donchian exit — it simply held long enough for the position to accumulate gains. The edge, when it exists, comes from patience rather than a precise exit mechanism.

What does the alpha/beta analysis show?

Near-zero beta vs both QQQ and SPY confirms that strategy returns are not driven by general market direction. This is expected for a long/short system where longs and shorts partially offset each other’s market exposure. Near-zero alpha means no excess returns are being generated either — performance is driven purely by the mechanical entry/exit structure.

Why is Donchian breakout harder on equities than commodities?

The original Turtle system was designed for commodity futures — crude oil, gold, currencies. These trend more persistently because supply/demand imbalances take months to resolve. Equity indices like QQQ mean-revert on short timescales because earnings, valuations, and institutional rebalancing constantly pull prices toward fair value. The 2024-2026 QQQ regime — sharp policy-driven spikes followed by fast recoveries — was particularly hostile to trend-following.

How would you improve the model?

The most promising improvement would be a volatility regime filter: skip all signals when realized volatility over the past 5 days is more than 2 standard deviations above its 60-day mean. This is observable before entry and would have excluded the two largest losing trades without any look-ahead bias. A second improvement would be entry refinement — waiting for a small intraday pullback toward the breakout level rather than entering at next-day open, improving average entry price and widening effective stop distance.

Assumptions & Reproducibility

  • All data from IBKR TWS via ShinyBroker, Regular Trading Hours only
  • Entry at next-day open after signal bar closes
  • Stop triggered at intraday low/high crossing stop level
  • No commissions or slippage modelled
  • All indicators use .shift(1) on lookback windows, no look-ahead bias
  • Data period: April 2024 to April 2026, 501 trading days
